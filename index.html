Open Education in Kenya

Mapping successful trends and remaining challenges

Education creates opportunity for individuals to contribute and compete locally and internationally. Therefore visualizing Kenyan education data reveals a dynamic picture of the present situation, as well as future challenges and opportunities. With fifty percent of the population under the age of 18 in 2009, it is critical to increase transparency in the process of investing in these future leaders. Explore the site to analyze how resources can be used to position Kenya to meet its Vision 2030 goal of having a “globally competitive, quality education and training for sustainable development”.[1]

High literacy rates throughout many districts are the result of a strong primary education system in Kenya. However, many gaps can be seen, with some literacy rates falling below 20% arid areas with lower populations. Predictably, literacy rates are higher where there is great access to schools. Literacy cannot improve without access to quality primary and secondary schools.

The introduction of the universal primary education policy in 2003 has been very successful, net enrolment rates increased from 80 percent to 91 percent in 2010. Free basic education is a compulsory right. According to the 2009 census 74 percent of the population has at least a primary education.However the quality of education differs dramatically between public and private schools, with the average pupil-teacher ratio in public primary schools almost 3 times higher than private.

Primary Attendance    Secondary Attendance

There is much greater regional variation in attending secondary school, than at primary. County enrollment rates range from 5-10 percent in the northern region and some parts of the Coast to around 50 percent in Kiambu and Nairobi. Most children passing the primary school exam can find a place in secondary school, but places have become competitive with less public secondary schools than private schools.

In Wajir, a district with an 84% poverty rate in the ASAL, family related reasons, such as "Had to work or help at home" and economic reasons like "No money for school costs", are frequently cited as the reason for never attending school.

Regardless of the school location and public education ensured as a universal right, other school-related costs or a family's economic insecurity can jeopardize the student attendance and retention.
