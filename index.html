Open Education in Kenya

Mapping successful trends and remaining challenges

Education creates opportunity for individuals to contribute and compete locally and internationally. Therefore visualizing Kenyan education data reveals a dynamic picture of the present situation, as well as future challenges and opportunities. With fifty percent of the population under the age of 18 in 2009, it is critical to increase transparency in the process of investing in these future leaders. Explore the site to analyze how resources can be used to position Kenya to meet its Vision 2030 goal of having a “globally competitive, quality education and training for sustainable development”.[1]

High literacy rates throughout many districts are the result of a strong primary education system in Kenya. However, many gaps can be seen, with some literacy rates falling below 20% arid areas with lower populations. Predictably, literacy rates are higher where there is great access to schools.

Primary Attendance

The introduction of the universal primary education policy in 2003 has been very successful, net enrolment rates increased from 80 percent to 91 percent in 2010. Free basic education is a compulsory right. According to the 2009 census 74 percent of the population has at least a primary education.

School locations are key to attendance rates, thus the national level educational attainment is not true in most counties situated in the arid and semi arid lands. There is a sizeable gap in educational attainment between rural and urban areas: 83 percent of the urban population has a primary education or higher compared to 70 percent in the rural areas.

There has also been large increases in the number of children enrolled in primary school in some of the more challenging counties such as Garissa, Turkana, Kericho, Mandera, Bungoma, Samburu, Nyeri and Siaya; although overall the enrolment rates remain far behind the national average and only about half of children in the arid and semi arid lands are enrolled in primary school.
