Exploring Kenyan Education

Visualizing education data in Kenya reveals a dynamic picture of the present situation as well as future challenges and opportunities.

With 50% of the population under the age of 18 it is increasingly important to increase education transparency as the country invests in these future leaders. Explore the site to see how resources are being used to reach Kenya's 2030 goal of creating “globally competitive, quality education and training for sustainable development”.[1]

    School locations are key to attendance rates, thus the national level educational attainment is not true in most counties situated in the arid and semi arid lands (ASAL). There is a sizeable gap in educational attainment between rural and urban areas: 83 percent of the urban population has a primary education or higher compared to 70 percent in the rural areas.

  • Reasons not attending School

    In Wajir, a district with an 84% poverty rate in the ASAL, family related reasons, such as 'Had to work or help at home' and economic reasons like 'No money for school costs', are frequently cited as the reason for never attending school.

    Regardless of the school location and public education ensured as a universal right, other school-related costs or a family"s economic insecurity can jeopardize the student attendance and retention.

  • Literacy by District

    High literacy rates throughout many districts are the result of a strong primary education system in Kenya. However, many gaps can be seen, with some literacy rates falling below 20% arid areas with lower populations. Predictably, literacy rates are higher where there is great access to schools. Literacy cannot improve without access to quality primary and secondary schools.
